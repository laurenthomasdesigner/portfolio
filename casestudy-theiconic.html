The Iconic —
a UX lens.

Australia's largest online fashion retailer has an exceptional product catalogue — but a number of friction points that chip away at the experience. This is my critical analysis of where The Iconic falls short, and what considered redesign could do about it.

Platform
theiconic.com.au

Note: This is an independent UX analysis produced as a personal project. I have no affiliation with The Iconic. All findings are based on my own heuristic evaluation, publicly available user reviews, and competitor benchmarking. Recommendations are speculative design proposals, not implemented changes.

About The Iconic
Australia and New Zealand's leading online fashion and lifestyle retailer. 60,000+ products, 1,000+ brands, free express delivery for Premier members. One of the region's most visited e-commerce platforms.
Why it matters
With such breadth of catalogue and a predominantly mobile audience, the quality of The Iconic's UX is directly tied to conversion, return rates, and long-term brand loyalty. Small friction points compound across millions of sessions.
My angle
As a UX designer with a fashion background, I approach this with both a user's eye and a designer's — understanding what the platform is trying to achieve commercially, and where the experience gets in the way.
Research approach

Combining expert review with real user evidence.

This analysis combines structured heuristic evaluation (applying Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics across key user journeys) with thematic analysis of 400+ publicly available customer reviews on Trustpilot and Reviews.io, benchmarked against best-in-class competitors including ASOS, Net-a-Porter, and Revolve.

I focused on four core journeys: browsing and discovery, the product page, checkout, and post-purchase — the areas with the highest drop-off risk and where the gap between The Iconic's catalogue strength and its UX quality is most visible.

Heuristic evaluation
Applied Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics across 4 core user journeys — identifying violations and rating their severity from cosmetic to critical.
User review analysis
Thematically analysed 400+ reviews across Trustpilot and Reviews.io — coding recurring pain points into problem categories.
Competitor benchmarking
Compared key flows against ASOS, Net-a-Porter, and Revolve — to identify where industry standard has moved ahead of The Iconic's current offering.
Journey mapping
Mapped the end-to-end experience across mobile and desktop — identifying moments of friction, confusion, and unmet expectation at each stage.

Problems identified

Seven friction points holding the experience back.

01
● High severity
Filter friction on a catalogue of 60,000+ items
The filter panel is one of the most used — and most frustrating — parts of the experience. On mobile the panel can collapse mid-session, losing selections. There's no way to save frequently used filter combinations. The number of filter options is overwhelming with no smart suggestion or prioritisation based on past behaviour. Filtered results don't persist if you navigate away and return.
  • Users abandon browsing sessions mid-filter
  • Power users have no shortcut to their usual preferences
  • Mobile experience significantly weaker than desktop
  • Discovery of new styles is impeded by filter overload
02
● High severity
Size and fit confidence is severely undermined
The Iconic stocks 1,000+ brands — each with their own sizing conventions. Yet there is no universal fit tool, no body measurement input, and sizing guides vary wildly in quality and format between brands. "Runs small / true to size / runs large" information is buried in the reviews section rather than surfaced prominently on the product page. For fashion shoppers, size anxiety is the single biggest barrier to purchase — and this experience exacerbates it.
  • High return rates linked to sizing uncertainty
  • Users rely on reviews rather than the product page for fit information
  • No body measurement tool or size calculator
  • Inconsistent size label formats across brands (AU/US/EU)
03
● High severity
Checkout payment flow creates unnecessary friction
The payment experience is a consistent source of frustration. Guest checkout routes users through an awkward PayPal flow where card details don't save. The range of payment options (PayPal, Afterpay, Zip, card) creates decision paralysis rather than convenience. Apple Pay and Google Pay — now standard on leading e-commerce platforms — are not prominently surfaced on mobile. Saved card management in account settings is unclear and hard to edit.
  • Cart abandonment at payment stage
  • Users express frustration with the PayPal guest flow specifically
  • BNPL options create cognitive load rather than reducing it
  • Competitors (ASOS, Net-a-Porter) offer cleaner one-page checkout
04
● High severity
Post-purchase experience is opaque and unsupported
Once an order is placed, users are largely left to figure out what happens next. The returns portal requires multiple clicks to find. Return status is not updated in real time. Refund timelines are not communicated at the point of return initiation — leading to users contacting support repeatedly. High-value returns (over $500) have been singled out for particularly slow processing. The gap between what users expect and what they receive is widest here.
  • Support contact volume driven by refund status queries
  • Returns portal 4+ clicks from homepage
  • No proactive notification at key return milestones
  • High-value return delays damage trust disproportionately
05
● Medium severity
Customer support is hard to find and hard to use
Multiple reviews cite the difficulty of finding a contact method — and once found, the experience disappoints. There is no prominently visible live chat during business hours. The help section is not surfaced at key friction moments (cart, order confirmation, post-purchase). When users do make contact, responses are slow or automated. The phone number — where it exists — has 20-minute hold times with no callback option.
  • "Hard to find the contact section" cited explicitly in reviews
  • No live chat visible on key transactional pages
  • Automated responses create frustration not resolution
  • Escalation path is unclear and unsignposted
06
● Medium severity
Colour filtering is too blunt for a fashion platform
Users can filter by broad colour (red, blue, green) but cannot distinguish between light and dark variants, neutrals, pastels or brights. A user looking for a "dusty rose" or "sage green" must scroll through all reds or greens. There's no pattern or texture filter (solid, stripe, floral, animal print). For a platform positioning itself as a premium fashion destination, this is a significant gap relative to competitors like Net-a-Porter and ASOS.
  • Users explicitly request light/dark sub-filtering
  • No pattern or texture filter
  • Casual and premium fashion searches are treated identically
  • Competitors offer more granular colour and material filtering
07
● Medium severity
Search and discovery defaults to commercial rather than personal
Sponsored products appear in search results without clear labelling — eroding trust in the relevance of results. There is no visual search (upload a photo to find matching items). Search autocomplete doesn't learn from individual browsing or purchase history. Editorial curation — which could differentiate The Iconic from pure-play search — is limited and not deeply integrated into the browsing journey.
  • Sponsored placement not clearly distinguished from organic results
  • No visual or camera-based search
  • Autocomplete is generic, not personalised
  • Editorial curation underused for discovery
Design recommendations

Seven targeted improvements — prioritised by impact.

Each recommendation is matched to a specific problem, with effort and impact considered. Quick wins should be shipped first to build momentum and user trust before tackling the more complex systemic changes.

01
Smart, persistent filter panel with saved preferences
Introduce a persistent quick-filter bar at the top of category pages showing the user's most-used filters (size, colour, price range). Allow filter sets to be saved as "My usual filters" and recalled in one tap. On mobile, redesign the filter sheet with a sticky "Apply" button and a clear count of results updating in real time. Filters should persist on back navigation.
Quick winDiscoveryMobile
02
Fit confidence system — from review to product page
Surface a standardised "fit rating" (runs small / true to size / runs large) directly below the size selector on every product page — drawn from review data. Add a body measurement tool to the user account (input height, weight, usual size in 3 brands) and generate a personalised size recommendation per product. Display sizing in all relevant label formats (AU/US/EU) consistently across every brand.
Longer termProduct pageReturns
03
One-page checkout with Apple Pay first
Redesign mobile checkout to surface Apple Pay and Google Pay as the primary option above the fold — reducing the journey to 2 taps. Restructure the payment selection UI to reduce cognitive load (one row of icons, clearly labelled). For returning users, default to their last used payment method with a single confirm action. Make guest checkout card entry save directly without requiring a PayPal account.
Quick winCheckoutMobile
04
Post-purchase hub with real-time return tracking
Create a dedicated "My Orders" experience accessible from the nav in one click — showing order status, return initiation (2 clicks maximum), and a real-time refund tracker with clear milestones (Returned received → Processing → Refund issued → In your account). Send proactive push/email notifications at each milestone. Explicitly communicate refund timelines at the point of return initiation, not after.
Post-purchaseTrustRetention
05
Contextual help — visible at moments of friction
Add a floating live chat widget on cart, checkout, order confirmation, and account pages during business hours. At other times, surface a prominent "Help" link that goes directly to a self-serve FAQ — not a general contact page. Add a callback request option on the phone support page. Display expected response times on all support channels so users aren't left in uncertainty.
Quick winSupportTrust
06
Granular colour and pattern filtering
Expand colour filtering to include light/dark/neutral/pastel/bright sub-categories within each colour family. Add a pattern filter (solid, stripe, floral, geometric, animal print, check). Allow multiple colour selections simultaneously. Consider a visual colour picker rather than text labels. Implement this first for the top 5 most-browsed categories (Dresses, Tops, Activewear, Jeans, Shoes) and expand from there.
DiscoveryFiltersFashion specificity
07
Visual search and personalised discovery
Introduce a camera/image search feature — users upload a photo (from Instagram, Pinterest, or their own camera) and The Iconic surfaces visually similar items. Integrate personalised autocomplete that draws on individual browse and purchase history. Clearly label sponsored products in search results as "Sponsored" to maintain trust in organic relevance. Invest in editorial-led discovery sections that reflect seasonal and style trends.
Longer termDiscoveryPersonalisation

Reflection

What this analysis showed me.

"The Iconic's catalogue is genuinely impressive. But great products deserve a great experience — and right now, the UX isn't living up to the ambition of the brand."

What struck me most was how many of the issues could be addressed relatively quickly. The checkout friction, the buried support, the blunt colour filter — none of these require a platform overhaul. They require prioritisation and a sharp user focus at the decision-making level. The harder, longer-term work around fit confidence and visual search is where The Iconic could genuinely differentiate — and where the fashion and UX intersection I care about most comes alive.

This kind of speculative analysis is something I find genuinely energising — taking a real platform, really using it, and asking: what would I change, and why?
