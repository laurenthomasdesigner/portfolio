The challenge

More data doesn't mean better decisions.

England's planning system generates enormous amounts of data — site boundaries, constraints, application histories, policy zones, flood risk, heritage designations. But access to data and the ability to use it meaningfully are very different things. The way planning data is presented is often inaccessible, inconsistent, and poorly suited to the decisions people actually need to make.

The discovery set out to understand who really needs to see planning data, what questions they're trying to answer with it, and whether current visualisation approaches are helping or hindering. That meant speaking to a wide range of users — from specialist planning officers to members of the public with no planning background at all.

Research methods
User interviews
In-depth interviews with planning officers, developers, PropTech providers, and members of the public — exploring how they currently access, interpret, and make decisions with planning data.
Contextual enquiry
Observed planning officers using existing map-based tools and data portals in their actual working environment — to understand real behaviour rather than what people report they do.
Stakeholder workshops
Facilitated workshops with DLUHC and Local Digital stakeholders to surface assumptions, align on scope, and ensure the research was focused on the right questions from the start.
Key findings
01
Data exists — but it's not designed for the decisions people need to make
Users across all groups could access planning data in some form. But it was rarely presented in a way that mapped to their actual question. Planning officers needed to cross-reference multiple systems. Developers needed to quickly assess viability. The public needed to understand what a proposal meant for their street. One dataset — many completely different needs.
02
Maps are assumed to be universal — but they're not equally accessible
The default response to planning data is a map. But many non-specialist users — particularly members of the public and community groups — struggled to interpret map-based interfaces. Visualisation choices were being made for technical users, leaving wider audiences behind and deepening existing inequalities in who can meaningfully engage with planning decisions.
03
Trust in data quality was a bigger barrier than format
Developers and planning officers alike expressed uncertainty about whether the data they were looking at was current and authoritative. No amount of improved visualisation could solve this — the underlying data pipeline and provenance needed to be addressed first. Users need to trust what they're seeing before they can act on it.

Outcomes

What the discovery unlocked.

The discovery reframed how the programme thought about visualisation — moving the conversation from "how do we display data better?" to "what decisions are people trying to make, and how does data need to be presented to support those?"

Design for decision-making, not data display
Users don't need more data — they need data presented in the context of the question they're trying to answer. This became a core design principle for the programme going forward.
Fix the data before designing the interface
Trust and data quality were more fundamental barriers than format or visualisation approach. Recommendations prioritised data pipeline and provenance work as a prerequisite.
Accessibility as a design requirement, not an add-on
The discovery surfaced how map-centric defaults excluded large portions of potential users. Inclusive design became embedded in subsequent visualisation work.
Reflection

What this discovery taught me.

"Planning data is political before it's technical. Understanding who gets to see it, in what form, and with what context, is a design decision with real consequences for who has a voice in shaping places."
