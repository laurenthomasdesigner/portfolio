The brief

Most local plans are still PDFs.

England's local plans are essential planning documents that guide future development, yet they are typically difficult to navigate, relying heavily on static PDFs and complex written content. In response to a national push towards digital planning, this discovery investigated how local plans could evolve into more accessible, interactive, and user-focused experiences.

Three how might we questions
01
How might we move from PDFs to more visual, web-based local plans?
02
How might we meet the needs of all end users to navigate, engage with and interact with local plan content at every stage?
03
How can we improve the use of interactive policy maps to visualise local plan content?

Design journey

From whiteboard to live government service.

01
Ideation & co-design
Facilitated co-design sessions with local planning authorities, planning officers, and statutory consultees to explore how policy data is understood and accessed. Used sketches, paper prototypes, and workshop activities to surface user needs and navigation behaviours.
02
Guidance design
Translated research into early guidance covering map usage, policy theme colour systems (WCAG-aligned), map layers, content structure, and technical data standards. These outputs informed published GOV.UK guidance (Feb 2025).
03
Prototyping & testing
Built and tested four prototypes spanning information architecture, interactive policy mapping, design standards, and data guidance. Conducted usability testing across three sessions with five local planning authorities of varying digital maturity.
04
Interactive map design
Designed the end-to-end interactive policy map, including Regulation 18, 19, and Adopted states, six policy themes, spatial constraints, search and filtering, polygon tools, and policy detail views.
Ideation & co-design

Starting with people, not pixels.

We began by engaging planning officers, GIS professionals, and statutory consultees to understand real-world workflows and constraints. I led the co-design sessions, using them to map system flows, explore early concepts, and test assumptions about the role of an interactive local plan.

Lo-fi guidance designs

Designing the standard before building the product.

The colour system defined six WCAG-validated policy themes — Housing (orange), Economy (purple), Design (pink/red), Environment & Sustainability (green), Infrastructure (brown), and Health & Wellbeing (blue) — now adopted as the recommended national standard. The map layer guidance established a consistent hierarchy for how spatial and non-spatial data should be structured, directly informing the interactive map prototype.

Policy detail

From map to policy in one click.

[ Add your policy detail page screenshot ]

A key user need identified in research was the ability to move seamlessly from seeing a policy on the map to reading its full text. The policy detail page design — shown here for H1 Housing Requirement — combines structured policy data (period, requirements) with full policy wording across clear sections: why the policy is needed, how it should be applied, and how outcomes will be measured.

Testing insights

Three things testing changed.

01
Area-wide policies must be queryable, not a layer
A key pivot from testing — non-spatial policies shouldn't sit on the map as a layer, but appear as a list when a user searches an area. This distinction shaped the entire map interaction model.
02
Standardised colours would save LPAs real time
LPAs confirmed they could adopt a colour standard — and that doing so would save them time. This validated the WCAG colour work and confirmed standardisation was feasible at national scale.
03
Images removed from content types
Planning officers didn't see images as useful within a local plan. Web content managers raised accessibility concerns. The prototype was iterated on the spot — a direct change driven by user feedback.
Reflection

This was the richest, most complex discovery I’ve led — holding desk research, market analysis, user needs, technical constraints, and national policy context simultaneously, while keeping the team focused on the questions that would actually move the programme forward. It also taught me something about the long tail of design work: the impact of a discovery often lands long after the project closes, in places you’re not always watching.
