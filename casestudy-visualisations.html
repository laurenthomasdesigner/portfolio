The challenge

Most local plans are still PDFs.

England's local plans — the documents that determine where homes, businesses and infrastructure can be built — are typically presented as text-heavy PDFs. There is limited and inconsistent use of visual content such as maps, tables, charts and images. The 2020 Planning for the Future white paper and the Levelling Up and Regeneration Bill both signal a requirement for local plans to become digital, interactive and accessible. This discovery set out to understand what that shift would actually require.

Three how might we questions
01
How might we move from PDFs to more visual, web-based local plans?
02
How might we meet the needs of all end users to navigate, engage with, and interact with local plan content at every stage?
03
How can we improve the use of interactive policy maps as a way to visualise local plan content?
Research approach

Six strands of research to understand the full picture.

32
Visual content audit
Audited visual content in 32 local plans across England — considering geographic spread, digital maturity, and plan stage. Landing pages, interactive maps, infographics, policy content.
27
GIS tools survey
Surveyed 27 LPAs on what GIS products they use — including digital maturity, skills, team sizes, and whether mapping is done in-house or outsourced.
20
Market analysis
Assessed 20 products LPAs use to draft, consult on, and publish local plan content — evaluating cost, technical requirements, integration, and ability to meet user needs.
11
User research sessions
A workshop with 6 national statutory consultees plus 5 semi-structured interviews with LPA GIS professionals — and co-design sessions exploring how users navigate planning data.
3
Frontrunner LPA sessions
Sessions with LPAs identified as frontrunners of digital local plans — to understand their approach, challenges encountered, and what LPA capability actually looks like in practice.
2
Co-prototyping with PropTech
Co-prototyping sessions with two mapping companies to test feasibility of our interactive map vision using existing market products — and what digital maturity implications that carried.
Design journey

From whiteboard to live government service.

Ideation & co-design
Ran collaborative co-design sessions with LPAs, planning officers, and statutory consultees. Whiteboard sketching, paper prototypes, and blue sticky note sessions to explore how different users navigate policy data — what they need to find, and how they'd want to access it.

What the audit showed

Inconsistency was the defining feature.

Auditing 32 local plans made one thing very clear: there was no standard. LPAs were bound by the structure of their own council websites, leading to creative but inconsistent use of design components. Most used text-heavy PDFs with limited visual content. The result was a fragmented landscape that failed end users at almost every stage.

01
Local plan landing pages lack clear structure and navigation
No consistency between local plans in how content was organised or signposted. LPAs were constrained by their council website structures. No examples of search functionality specifically built for local plan content were found across the 32 audited.
02
The user journey to interactive maps was clunky and unclear
Interactive maps were often hard to find — linked to without context or instructions, and requiring users to move to an entirely different platform with no continuity. The journey from any stage of plan-making to map content was poorly signposted.
03
Interactive maps looked completely different plan to plan
Colours were used inconsistently — a clear shared visual language was entirely absent. For any user viewing multiple local plans, this created real friction and made national comparison of planning policy almost impossible.
04
The challenge is less about products — and far more about capability
The existing PropTech products LPAs use are capable of a higher standard than is currently present in most local plans. The gap is not primarily technical — it is about the support, skills, and resources LPAs need. This reframed the design challenge significantly.

Lo-fi — guidance page designs

Designing the standard before building the product.

A key insight from research was that LPAs needed guidance before they needed a tool. These lo-fi designs — covering colour standards, map layer hierarchy, content types, and technical data guidance — were developed, iterated, and tested before moving to high-fidelity. They became the basis for the live GOV.UK guidance published in February 2025.

The colour key (left) defined 6 WCAG-validated policy theme colours — Housing (orange), Economy (purple), Design (pink/red), Environment & sustainability (green), Infrastructure (brown), Health & wellbeing (blue) — that are now the recommended standard for every LPA in England. The map layers guidance (right) established the standardised layer hierarchy that fed directly into the interactive map prototype.

The prototypes

Four prototypes to test the vision.

Following ideation and research synthesis, four prototypes were built and taken to user testing with LPA users across England.

01
Site structure for an online local plan
A standardised structure LPAs could follow on their council website or microsite — showing a zoomed-out view of a whole online local plan including landing page, all policies, interactive maps, evidence base, plan history, help, and contact. Tested across three different hosting approaches.
02
Interactive policy map vision
A high-fidelity prototype testing the vision for an interactive policy map — 6 standardised policy themes and colours, a standardised base map, non-spatial policy handling, postcode search, flood risk constraint layers, Reg 18/19/Adopted plan versioning with circular key diagram to adopted polygon progression.
03
Style guidelines
Example guidelines forming part of a wider style guide — covering standardised layer hierarchy, maps at different plan-making stages, standardised policy theme colours validated against WCAG accessibility standards on three base map colours, and guidance on non-map content types.
04
Technical data guidance
Prototypes of technical guidance pages to support LPAs with data-related tasks through the plan-making journey — each with a clear contents page, minimum tools and skills required, different options for different tools, and step-by-step guidance tested across low, mid, and high digital maturity LPAs.
Hi-fi — interactive policy map

Every state, every user journey.

The interactive policy map prototype was designed across every state and user journey — from the default adopted view with all 6 policy theme layers, to postcode search showing policies affecting a specific area, to the historical Reg 18 consultation view with circular key diagram markers, to polygon drawing mode for site boundary searches.

Policy detail page

From map to policy in one click.

A key user need identified in research was the ability to move seamlessly from seeing a policy on the map to reading its full text. The policy detail page design — shown here for H1 Housing Requirement — combines the structured policy data (period, requirements) with the full policy wording across clear sections: why the policy is needed, how it should be applied, and how outcomes will be measured.

User testing

3 sessions · 7 users · 5 Local Planning Authorities.

Tested with planning officers, planning and GIS professionals, and a web content manager from Leeds City Council, Greater Cambridge Shared Planning, Norfolk County Council, Cheshire, and others — representing low, mid, and high digital maturity LPAs.

01
The site structure was validated across all three hosting types
All LPA users validated the proposed site structure as appropriate and workable — regardless of their current hosting approach. This gave us confidence it could work as a genuine national standard.
02
The interactive map was described as "simple and easy to use"
All LPA users validated the interactive policy map as feasible within their existing mapping products. Broad agreement on the concept of consistent policy themes — but users flagged the need for further colour testing before finalising specific values.
03
Area-wide policies should be queryable — not a map layer
A key design decision from testing: area-wide policies should not be visualised as a layer on the map, but should be 'queryable' — appearing as a list when a user searches an area. This distinction between spatial and non-spatial policy became a central principle of the map design.
04
Standardised colours would save LPAs time
LPA users confirmed they could change their existing colour approach to follow a proposed standard — and that doing so would save them time. This validated the WCAG colour accessibility work and confirmed standardisation was feasible at scale.
05
Images removed from content types after testing feedback
Planning officers did not feel 'Images' were useful within the local plan content context; web content managers raised accessibility concerns. The prototype was iterated to remove this content type — a direct change driven by user insight.
Recommendations

MVP baseline standards — and what comes next.

Baseline 01
Standardised colours for map layers
LPAs should use standardised colours for map layers based on 6 policy themes. Colours validated against WCAG accessibility requirements on 3 base map colours. Now live as official GOV.UK guidance.
Baseline 02
Standardised layer hierarchy
LPAs should follow a standardised layer hierarchy for interactive maps — validated in testing with the proposed order confirmed as correct by LPA users.
Baseline 03
Search by location and user-drawn boundary
Users should be able to search by postcode, use 'my location', and draw a site boundary — designed and tested in the prototype, now reflected in the national planning data map.
Baseline 04
Geospatial data specification for policies
LPAs should follow a specification for geospatial data connected to policies — foundational to delivering consistent map layer colours and hierarchy nationally.
Beyond baseline
Standardised online local plan structure
Validated in testing but requiring further work with web content teams before becoming a standard — with further research needed into technical and procurement implications.
Priority gap
Standards for constraint layers and line styling
Standards for constraints, site allocations, and boundary line styling are needed but not yet prioritised for research — flagged by LPAs as important and scoped for the next phase.
Reflection

What this discovery taught me.

"The hardest design challenge here wasn't the interface — it was helping a diverse group of LPAs with completely different tools, teams and capabilities imagine the same future. I only recently found out the guidance went live — and that feeling of knowing your work shapes how planning decisions are made across the whole of England is something quite extraordinary."

This was the richest, most complex discovery I've led — holding desk research, market analysis, user needs, technical constraints, and national policy context simultaneously, while keeping the team focused on the questions that would actually move the programme forward. It also taught me something about the long tail of design work: the impact of a discovery often lands long after the project closes, in places you're not always watching.
