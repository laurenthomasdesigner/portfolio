The programme

Modernising England's planning system — one piece at a time.

Local Plans are the foundation of England's planning system — legally required documents that determine where homes, businesses and infrastructure can be built. But the process of creating them is slow, inconsistent, and often contested. The Digital Planning Programme was set up to change that, working across data, software, plan-making and policy.

I worked across two separate pieces of work within the programme — first as lead on a discovery into planning data visualisations, then as lead on a full alpha into how Local Planning Authorities (LPAs) select and publicise development sites. Both involved working with a multidisciplinary team across design, policy, tech, and data.

01
Discovery

Planning Data Visualisations

Planning generates enormous amounts of data — site boundaries, constraints, application histories, policy zones — but the way this data is presented is often inaccessible, inconsistent, and poorly suited to the decisions people actually need to make. This discovery set out to understand who needs to see planning data, what they need from it, and what kinds of visualisation genuinely help versus overwhelm.

Key findings
01
Data exists — but isn't designed for the decisions people need to make
Users across all groups could access planning data in some form, but it was rarely presented in a way that mapped to their actual question. Planning officers needed to cross-reference multiple systems. Developers needed to quickly assess viability. The public needed clarity on what a proposal meant for their street. One dataset — many completely different needs.
02
Maps are assumed to be universal — but they're not equally accessible
The default response to planning data is a map, but many non-specialist users — particularly community groups and members of the public — struggled to interpret map-based interfaces. Visualisation choices were being made for technical users, leaving wider audiences behind and deepening existing inequalities in planning participation.
03
Trust in data quality was a bigger barrier than format
Developers and planning officers alike expressed uncertainty about whether data was current and authoritative. Visualisation improvements alone wouldn't solve this — the underlying data pipeline needed to be addressed alongside any front-end changes for users to actually rely on what they were seeing.
02
Alpha

Selecting & Publicising Sites

Identifying, assessing and allocating sites is one of the most time-consuming and costly parts of the plan-making process. Practice among Local Planning Authorities is uneven — due to imprecise, poorly understood, or in some parts of the process non-existent guidance, methodologies and standards. Failure to get it right can lead to local plans being found 'unsound', additional cost for LPAs, and costs for developers who invest in sites that are later overturned.

This alpha focused on two of the highest-value ideas identified in discovery: standardising the site identification form and the site assessment methodology.

The prototypes
01
Step-by-step playbook
Structured guidance for LPAs on how to run the full site selection process — addressing the absence of a clear, centrally produced methodology.
02a
Site identification form
A standardised online form for landowners and developers to submit sites for consideration during the call for sites — reducing inconsistency and manual processing by LPAs.
02b
Hosting proof of concept
Explored different technical approaches to hosting the site identification form, including centralised vs. federated data models and security considerations.
03
Site assessment methodology
A standardised, government-backed framework for how LPAs should assess submitted sites — aiming to improve consistency and reduce challenges at Examination in Public.
04
Site information view
A public-facing view of assessed sites — making the outcomes of site selection visible and searchable for communities, developers, and planning inspectors.
05
Assessment outputs template
A standardised template for communicating site assessment outputs — making results consistent, auditable, and usable for Examination in Public.
Research approach
Policy explorations
Planning experts documented policy requirements, guidance, and best practice examples for site assessment methodologies across different LPA contexts.
Design explorations
The design team built and iterated prototypes, testing with LPAs, councillors, PINS, developers, and landowners across multiple research sprints.
Tech explorations
The tech team investigated how GIS data could be collected and displayed, building end-to-end proofs of concept to test feasibility and viability.
Data explorations
The data team investigated the benefits and drawbacks of using JSON Schema to configure dynamic form fields and how submitted site data could be stored and standardised.
Who we engaged
01
Local planners
Mostly from rural and mixed rural/urban LAs, low to medium digital maturity — to understand local feasibility and the flexibility needed when implementing the prototypes. These LPAs were underrepresented in discovery and brought essential practical context to the alpha.
02
Developers & landowners
To understand their experience of the site identification and submission process — what they currently provide, what's difficult, and how a standardised form would change their workflow and the quality of submissions.
03
Planning inspectors (PINS)
To assess whether the prototype outputs would hold up at Examination in Public — and understand what evidence inspectors actually need to be confident in an LPA's site assessment decisions.
04
Councillors, web managers & policy owners
Councillors gave us the political dimension — a perspective often missing from digital planning work. "What is missing is the role of councillors — they are a huge influencer in making the 30-month timetable work." — a Local Planner

6 key recommendations

What we said should happen next.

After 24 interviews, 4 workshops and 30+ tested assumptions, we delivered six concrete recommendations to DLUHC — grounded in evidence and designed to move the programme forward with confidence.

01
Alpha on Minimum Evidence Base
Prioritise a focused alpha on what constitutes the minimum evidence base for sound site selection — addressing one of the most contested areas in Local Plan examinations.
02
Create the site selection step-by-step playbook
DLUHC should prioritise centrally producing the step-by-step playbook for site selection — including a standardised site assessment methodology, instructions for the site identification form, and data standards.
03
Investigate tech hosting models
Further exploration is needed into different hosting scenarios and market involvement. A dashboard solution was identified as the optimal technical approach — providing a single source of data truth and reducing data validation overhead for LPAs.
04
In-depth policy research on site allocations
Deeper policy research into the current state of site allocations content and user needs — to align this work with the wider Local Plan components data standardisation programme.
05
Move to Beta — in priority order
Progress beta phases into the products and tools that support LPAs to complete site selection — in order: (a) site identification form, (b) templates for communicating assessment outputs, (c) online dashboard.
06
Outcomes-based roadmap delivered
The alpha concluded with a speculative future user journey and outcomes-based roadmap — giving the programme a clear view of what a mature, digitally-supported site selection process could look like end-to-end.
What leading looks like

More than doing the work — shaping how it gets done.

Being Interaction Design Lead across both phases meant owning the full shape of the work — from research strategy to final recommendations — while operating as a consultant in a complex multi-team government environment.

  • Led the research strategy and prioritisation for both the discovery and the alpha
  • Designed and facilitated user interviews, workshops and prototype testing sessions across multiple sprints
  • Led synthesis and translated qualitative data from 24 interviews into clear, actionable findings
  • Collaborated with planning policy experts, tech leads and data specialists in a genuinely multidisciplinary team
  • Managed stakeholder relationships with DLUHC, PINS, and senior programme leads
  • Presented findings and recommendations at programme level, advocating for user needs in policy-driven decisions
  • Delivered to embedded-team standard as a consultant — building trust and context quickly across both phases
Reflection

What two phases of government design taught me.

"The planning system is dense, political, and deeply human. The most valuable thing I brought wasn't a method — it was the ability to make people feel heard, and then translate what they said into something a programme could actually act on."

Working across discovery and alpha phases in a government context — with real policy stakes, multidisciplinary teams, and a system that affects every person in England — pushed me to operate at a different level. I learned that good interaction design in this space isn't just about the screens. It's about building the evidence base that earns trust, shaping the questions before anyone starts building, and making complexity navigable for the people who have to make decisions with it.
